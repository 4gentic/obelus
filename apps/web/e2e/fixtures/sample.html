Notes on the Margin Note

The marginal gloss is the oldest reading apparatus in the European book. It predates the printed footnote by four centuries, and predates the citation by longer still. A reader who marks a page in 2026 is reaching for the same affordance a Carolingian monk reached for in 820: a small, parallel column of disagreement.

What the gutter does

Three things, in roughly this order. First, it preserves the body text intact — the gloss does not rewrite, it argues alongside. Second, it surfaces disagreement at the line where the disagreement happens, rather than deferring it to the end of the chapter. Third, it makes the reader's labour visible: the gloss is signed, dated, and physically present.

The margin is the place where reading becomes writing.

This last point is the one that contemporary review tools most often forget. A modal dialog over the page is not a margin; it is an interruption. A comment thread filed below the document is not a margin either; it is exile. The margin's whole virtue is its physical adjacency.

What the gutter is not

It is not the place for the body of an argument. Glosses are short by design. A reviewer who finds themselves writing three paragraphs in the margin has discovered that the disagreement is not local — it belongs in a separate document, addressed to the author rather than to the page. The medieval scribes understood this; they ran longer commentaries as their own works, citing the original by chapter rather than by line.

It is also not a substitute for revision. A page covered in marginalia is a page asking to be rewritten, not a page that has been improved. Bourdieu noted somewhere that the proliferation of glosses on a text is a sign of its declining authority — readers no longer trust the body to speak for itself. The same dynamic operates in document review software: heavy annotation often signals that the document needs a structural rewrite, not more comments.

A note on tooling

The reviewer's letter and the marginal gloss serve different purposes. The letter is addressed to the author and summarises a position; the gloss is addressed to the page and registers a single, local disagreement. A tool that conflates them — that asks the reviewer to write the letter in the margin, or to scatter the letter's claims across forty disconnected glosses — produces neither well.
